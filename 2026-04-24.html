THE BENCH

The last open rehearsal before Pac-12 opens for real

Saturday's Spring Game is BSU's final public audition — and the real competition isn't at quarterback.

The Lede: Maddux Madsen owns QB1. The Spring Game tomorrow is about everything below him.

Warmups at Albertsons Stadium begin at 1:30 PM Saturday. Kickoff at 2:10 PM MDT. Blue versus Orange, modified scoring for explosive plays — runs of 12-plus yards, passes of 16-plus — and a defense that has six starters to replace, four of them in the secondary. That is the honest shape of this roster heading into the Pac-12 era, which officially begins July 1. Spencer Danielson signed his extension through 2030 on April 20, so at least the anchor holds.

The storyline that matters most is the offensive line. Kage Casey anchored that left tackle spot for years. Tyler Ethridge arrived from Colorado State-Pueblo — a D2 program — and Danielson said publicly that Ethridge has "immersed himself" in the team's culture and is performing at a high level. If Ethridge holds the job through fall camp, it validates how BSU scouts outside the Power conference pipeline. The same experiment runs on defense: Mikaio Edward transferred from Central Washington and is the litmus test for whether cross-division recruiting holds up when the opposition gets bigger.

Of the 18 transfers integrated this spring, many exist to patch the 11 departed starters. Jason Steele and Zander Esty are competing at center now that Mason Randolph is gone. Dylan Riley will get looks in the backfield. Jayden Virgin-Morgan's daily edge battles with Matt Wagner were called "some of the most competitive repetitions witnessed on the West Coast this spring" by Danielson — which, if you're keeping score, means the defense might have its identity before the offense settles its interior. And Rasean Jones, the true freshman receiver Danielson called a "football guy" with a "bright" future on The Blue, is one to track when the ball moves fast.

The Treasure Valley subplot: Zeke Martinez, true freshman out of Meridian, generating real buzz as the backup with a high ceiling. Max Cutforth, Nampa product, is his competition. Two local kids competing for the same job, on the blue turf that turns 40 years old this weekend. That is the kind of number that makes a Spring Game feel like more than a scrimmage.

Six defensive starters to replace, four of them in the secondary — the Pac-12 era starts with real questions.

Boise painted the poles and the internet named it

The city followed the letter of the flag ban — then picked up a brush.
The community named it before the city did — and the photo already said everything.

The story that broke out of Boise this week and landed on national timelines started with a law and ended with a paint can. Idaho enacted a ban on non-official flags at government buildings. Boise complied — removed the Pride flags, then painted the flagpoles themselves in rainbow colors, along with crosswalks and public art installations around City Hall. The community named what happened before any city official did: malicious compliance. That term emerged organically on Reddit and Bluesky, not from a press release. The photos circulated fast.

The Idaho Family Policy Center and the Idaho Freedom Foundation both pushed back, arguing the city used public funds to dodge the spirit of the law. That argument is real and it will continue. But the image — a flagpole, stripped of its flag, painted instead — is already doing its own rhetorical work regardless of where you stand on the underlying law.

The civic anchor that grounds all of this is quieter but worth watching: a Boise-area daycare sued the state over the Medical Freedom Act, arguing the law prevents standard public-health practices like sending sick children home. Abstract culture-war legislation, meet the parent at morning drop-off. That lawsuit is the flag debate in a room with actual children in it.

Record April, freeze watch, and one sweet Saturday window

MOOD OF THE SKY: RELENTLESS.

Boise just made history and not the warm kind. April 2026 has logged 3.87 inches of precipitation — a new all-time record, surpassing marks that stood for over a century. April 22 alone dropped 0.87 inches in a single day. January came in at 0.73 inches, March at 0.79 — near-desert dry, both of them. Then April showed up with a suitcase and refused to leave. The Boise River is running high and cold, and some Greenbelt sections are seeing minor ponding.

Today: high of 47°F, which is nearly 15 degrees below the seasonal average of 63°F. Northwest winds 9 to 18 mph, humidity sitting at 79%, and that wind chill this morning felt like 34°F. This afternoon clears out and the sun shows up — but the thermometer doesn't follow. Dottie didn't want to come back inside this morning, which usually means the pressure is still unstable. She was right.

Here is the thing about Saturday: the NWS Freeze Watch runs 4:00 AM to 9:00 AM MDT. If you put anything tender in the ground during that warm stretch, cover it tonight before you go to bed — not Saturday morning, because by then it's already happening. Once that watch lifts, Saturday becomes the best outdoor window of the weekend. High of 60°F, mostly sunny, winds light out of the south-southwest. The Spring Game kicks off at 2:10 PM and the lawn chair crowd will be fine. Sunday runs 55°F with patchy frost before 7 AM again, but the afternoon is clean. Get outside Saturday between 10 AM and 4 PM. The sky is giving you exactly one gift this weekend — take it.

THE WAY I SEE IT

Partisan potholes and a budget you didn't vote on

SB 1356 passed both the Idaho House and Senate in two hours on April 1, 2026. Two hours. The Ada County Highway District — ACHD — is responsible for road maintenance, traffic signals, and infrastructure across Ada County. Its commissioners currently run in nonpartisan races. Under SB 1356, which has passed both chambers and is awaiting Governor Little's signature, those races become partisan elections. The strongest version of the case for this bill is that all government should be accountable to a party platform. The honest counter is that a pothole does not have a party affiliation, and the people fixing it probably shouldn't need one either. Local officials and city council members have broadly said so.

HB 842 is the bill fewer people are talking about and the one with the larger financial footprint. It removes cities' ability to collect forgone property tax revenue without going to a ballot initiative. Boise's FY 2026 General Fund sits at $331.6 million — a 1.2% increase from the prior year. The city's proposed 7.6% property tax increase funds police technology modernization and a rebate program for vulnerable homeowners. The Idaho Freedom Foundation has called that increase a betrayal of taxpayer trust. Governor Little's Enduring Idaho plan, announced April 22, frames these restrictions as fiscal responsibility. The legislature adjourned April 14.

Three numbers worth watching before this is settled: the final property tax rate Boise can actually collect after HB 842 takes effect, the cost of ACHD commissioner races once party infrastructure gets involved, and how many nonpartisan infrastructure boards are left in Ada County after the 2026 cycle. Measure the thing — not the press release about the thing.

Twenty-one gone. Nine left. Bring a raffle ticket.
West Ada students built 30 doghouses, and the public didn't even get a shot at most of them.
— A dispatch from the archives —

The scarcity is the story. Thirty doghouses — designed and built by West Ada School District CTE Residential Construction Level 1 students — went on display at The Village at Meridian, 3597 E Monarch Sky Ln. Twenty-one of them sold before the public event opened. Nine remain at $500 each. CALLING IT: if you show up this weekend expecting to browse thirty options, reset your expectations now. The raffle is the move most families won't know about unless someone tells them. Consider yourself told. Contact Jenna Englund at BCASWI (208-995-2885) for details. Hours run approximately 10 AM to 9 PM through Sunday.

Proceeds benefit the BCASWI Trade Education and Training Scholarship Fund, including the Mia Englund Memorial Scholarship — a $2,911 award for students pursuing construction careers. The CTE pipeline matters in the Treasure Valley right now, and the sold-before-you-arrived reality is the proof of concept. Kyle would ask how much it cost. The answer is $500, which is also the answer to what it costs to give a high schooler's first major build a home.
